Y2K and Action Plan for your Office - Corbin Ball Associates - the Meetings Technology Professionals

Associates Computers have infiltrated into every corner of our lives. Every time you pick up your phone, use your credit card, turn on light switch, and reserve an airline seat or hotel room, you are using an intricate and interconnected web of computer systems. This web may develop some significant breaks in this coming year due to the Y2K bug -- the inability for many computers and microprocessors to process dates after 1999. Potentially a wide variety of systems failures will occur. The "hangover" on the morning of January 1, 2000 due to these failures may be much greater than a normal New Year's Day. This article will focus on the impact on your office and what you can do to prepare -- how to make sure your computers, office systems, and applications are not bitten by Y2K. Will your computer work? The good news is that Mac-based computers are compliant at least through the year 2040. However, if you own one of the 260 million PC-based computers distributed throughout the world, you have three potential failure points: 1.) at the hardware level, 2) at the operating system and 3) at the software level. 1. The Hardware: The BIOS chip in your PC controls the hardware including your system clock. 79% of pre-1997 PCs have BIOS chips that are not Y2K compliant. How can you check your system? Your computer vendor can tell you. There is also a free utility through NSTL (http://www.nstl.com/) that will test your BIOS and other hardware system to see if it will work properly. 2. The Operating System: Macs are compliant. Window 95 and 98 are Y2K-compliant with patches (see Microsoft site at end of the article). The older DOS and Window 3.0 and 3.1 will have problems. Fortunately, these can often be corrected with help. (See the above NSTL site listed for info.) 3. Software: Applications are the trickiest part. Many programs such as spreadsheets and databases rely heavily on dates as do many meeting planning software programs. Most major software companies will have patches or new versions that will solve it. A quick review of some of the meeting planning packages: Ajenis (Plansoft) fully compliant Complete Event Manager (EKEBA) fully compliant Core Discovery (McGettigan) fully compliant Event Planner Plus (Certain Software): can process dates after 1999, however, operates on 16-bit platform which is technically not Y2K compliant Gold 2.0 (ISIS) fully compliant iMIS (ASI) fully compliant MeetingPro (Peopleware): releases after Nov. 97 fully compliant MeetingTrack (Phoenix Solutions) : latest version (4.5) fully compliant; previous versions can receive a free patch. Will your office equipment work? Embedded microprocessor chips are everywhere -- more than 9 billion programmable microcontroller chips were produced in 1997 alone! Fortunately, most office machines do not track the year, and, therefore, are not prone to Y2K problems. However, for the ones that do (such as in security systems, some elevators, etc.) there is a likelihood that they will perform erratically. Checking with the product vendor is the best way to get information on how to correct the problem. Will your supply chain work? This is the scariest part of the problem. We all rely on highly interconnected supply and distribution systems. The electrical power you use to turn on the light, for example, is part of a North American power grid controlled by mainframe computers, embedded systems and microcontrollers. In July 1996, a single, local power outage in Idaho caused loss of power in 15 western states, Canada and Mexico. With a system as vast and complex as this, some localized Y2K-induced power outages are almost a certainty. The question is, will multiple local power failures ripple through and bring down regional power and affect large numbers of people? If so, for how long? Electricity is just one of the links in the supply chain. Others include transportation, (railways, airlines, truck distribution networks), telecommunications. A chain is only as strong as the weakest link, and there are numerous potential breaking points. Unfortunately, there are no easy answers here. There are billions of lines of computer code to check and there are billions of embedded systems chips out there. There is simply not enough time to check them all. Several city, local government and civil defense groups are starting to hold meetings on the potential local impact and I would recommend checking them out. What about your vendors and suppliers? It is wise to ask your suppliers about their Y2K compliance plans. What will you do if these supplier goods or services are delayed by a month or two? What about the hotels, convention centers, travel agencies, airlines and other suppliers that you are working with? It is likely that there will be at least some business disruption. It main questions are: how much and how long? More information: As the deadline approaches, there will be a swell of discussion about this topic that may border on hysteria. However, it is definitely an area where awareness, a personal risk assessment, and correction is warranted. Some recommended web site reading include: www.yardeni.com A very complete site with an emphasis on economic impact www.y2ktoday.com Lots of links and a great small business evaluation section www.year2000.com Articles and the latest Y2K news www.microsoft.com/technet/topics/year2k Microsoft's product info and Y2K news source www.vendor2000.com List compliance status for more than 125,000 products and 3000 vendors. No one knows exactly what will happen on January 1, 2000, but at least we know exactly when it will happen - and that time is rapidly approaching. Now is the time for education and preparation.
